Learning Objectives

This tutorial is about the ways that genetic and epigenetic data are encoded and stored. While we may allude to how this data is analyzed, we will primarily be dealing with file types and data structures. By the end of this section, we expect that you will be able to:

  1. Identify common file types and what types of data are stored in them
  2. Describe some technical limitations of converting genetic and epigenetic information into computer-friendly formats
  3. Find publicly available data from online repositories and download them to your local computer

The evolution of techniques to read DNA will be covered in class next week. We’re introducing the topic now to give you an idea of the complexities and help you contextualize what you’re about to learn.

Representing the raw sequence

At its simplest, genetic data is an ordered sequence of nucleotides. So, the most obvious way to encode genetic data is to simply write out the nucleotide at each position of the genome. Unsurprisingly, this is how the first geneticists and biochemists recorded DNA sequences.

However, reading the entire genome base by base is hard. The first geneticists and biochemists did not do this. Instead, they focused on specific genes and wrote out the sequences by hand (reading them from a gel, but more on this in class).

After transcripts of genes were widely available, we didn’t transition immediately to sequencing entire chromosomes or genomes. Much of our genetic sequences are conserved, or exactly the same, not just across people or individuals of the same species, but also across species. So for a long time, people only looked for variation. Instead of obtaining a genetic sequence, they assessed which base was present at a position that is known to vary between people.

We didn’t try to sequence the entire genome for a long time because we believed in “junk DNA.” These assumptions, that DNA doesn’t vary much from person to person and that any DNA that isn’t coding DNA is meaningless, have not held up. So nowadays, we do a lot more sequencing and of a lot more things.

Even as we transition to longer sequences of DNA, it is important to remember that we don’t have magnifying glasses powerful enough to read DNA. Instead, we use methods with varying levels of accuracy to decide what we think the sequence of bases is.

FASTQ

The most common way to represent raw sequencing data is with a FASTQ file. The sequence for each read generated by the sequencing machine is stored in the same way as in a FASTA file, but with two additional lines encoding the quality of the sequence. The quality scores are where the “Q” in FASTQ comes from.

In two weeks we will be using data from the 1000 Genomes project to learn how to use PLINK and determine the global ancestry of samples. Header lines of a FASTQ file begin with the “@” character instead of “>”. In a FASTQ file header lines function as read identifiers and generally contain information like the sample ID, the read group, the flowcell, etc. Different sequencers have specific read identifier formats.

Just as in a FASTA file, the line following the identifier contains the raw sequence. Unlike the FASTA, this line is followed by a separator14 (“+”) and a bunch of alphanumeric characters on the fourth line representing the sequence quality.

The most common type of score to be used for this line is called a Phred score. A Phred score is a log transformation of the probability that there was an error for a given base (\(q=-10\log_{10}(p)\)). That error probability (\(p\)) is based on the surrounding florescence peaks of the sequencing machine. This means that a higher value Phred score represents a higher quality sequence and a lower error probability.

You might notice that these scores are numeric and the quality score line in the FASTQ file is letters and special characters. This is because it takes more memory to store a two digit number like “10” than it does to store a single character. In FASTQ files, the scores are mapped to characters by using a range of the ASCII characters in order.

Representing the aligned sequence

While simply representing the raw sequence with a FASTA or FASTQ file is a good start, most of the time we don’t work with genetic data in that format. In fact, the data that we’re going to use next week to practice variant calling isn’t even available as FASTQ files. This is because the raw reads don’t tell us where in the genome a sequence comes from (or where it maps to). To encode this information, we need a file format capable of encoding the alignment of the data.

SAM

The most simple version of this format happens to share my name! SAM (Sequence Alignment/Map) files contain genetic information aligned to a reference genome.

Just like with FASTA and FASTQ files, the header lines of SAM files contain important information about the genetic data within. Unlike raw data files, however, all the header lines in a SAM file must be at the beginning of the file, before any lines representing genetic data. Header lines begin with “@” and have very specific rules about how they should be formatted. We’ll go over the most important ones in a moment.

The alignment of each segment to the reference is represented by a single line with at least 11 tab-delimited fields. To be fully honest, I almost never look at these lines manually because there is little to be learned from them.

If you are consumed by curiosity, here is a simplified version of the SAM documentation of the 11 mandatory alignment fields:

BAM

A BAM is a binary SAM file, this means that the information from the SAM file as described above has been compressed so that the file takes up less room. This is incredibly important, because high coverage whole-genome BAM files generally take up more than 100GB even with compression. In fact, there is an additional file format with even more compression called CRAM. Both BAM and CRAM files generally require index files (BAI and CRAI files) to enable retrieving alignments for a portion of the genome without traversing the whole alignment.

Representing the variants

FASTQs and BAMs generally contain all of the genetic data for an individual. As we mentioned, this makes them large files that are difficult to process and store. It also means that it is easier to identify individuals in those files than it is if we simply have a file with the genotypes of an individual at variant sites. For this reason, most open access genetic data is in VCF or PLINK file format.

Both file formats are also common ways to represent genotype data from microarray chips.

VCF

VCF (Variant Call Format) files are the most common way to represent genotypes after variant calling. The VCF files for the 1000G data we’ve been looking at can be found on the FTP site.

These files also take a long time to download, although they are significantly smaller than BAM files, so you can find the downloaded files in Sam’s class directory (/u/home/class/hg236/hg236-03/week_3_PLINK/1000G_VCFs).

Like BAM files, VCF files too have a large number of header lines at the beginning of the file that describe how the variant calls were made and determine the organization of the fields. They begin with “#”. Using bcftools, the analogue of samtools for VCFs and BCFs (binary VCFs), you can view just the header lines of one of the VCFs on Hoffman2:

Representing epigenetic sequencing data

Epigenetics can refer to many different mechanisms of information transmission that is independent of changes to nucleotides. The strict definition includes only heritable phenotype changes, but it is often used to describe functional changes to the genome regardless of whether they are inherited. This can include gene expression, alternative splicing of genes, DNA methylation, chromatin modifications, or histone modifications. When we talk about modifications, such as acetylation, methylation, ubiquitylation, phosphorylation, or sumoylation, we are referring to the addition of a protein or protein group to the chromatin, histone or DNA. The name of this protein or protein group precedes the -ylation term.

Some of these epigenetic modifications can be studied with modified sequencing techniques. For example, DNA methylation is the addition of a methyl group to cytosine. Bisulfite treatment will change a cytosine to uracil (an RNA nucleotide), but will leave 5-methylcytosine (cytosine with a methyl group on the 5’ end) unaffected. This means that after DNA is treated, it can be sequenced to determine which nucleotides were methylated and which were not.

Other epigenetic modifications are studied by a combination of sequencing and other techniques. For example, ChIP-seq is chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by sequencing. This method gives us information about where specific transcription factors are binding. ChIP-seq refers to a technique: knowing that data is coming from ChIP-seq doesn’t tell you what epigenetic data is being obtained in the way that bisulfite sequencing does. You can immunoprecipitate many different proteins, so it’s important to note which one was targeted. ChIP-seq aims to only sequence portions of the genome that were proximate to the targeted protein.

RNA-sequencing is a very well-known source of epigenetic data, likely because it is relatively easy to obtain and data analysis is fairly straightforward to interpret.

How is epigenetic data different from genetic data?

If we are using sequencing technology, we use the same file types to encode epigenetic data as we would with genetic data. However, our assumptions are different. Multiple reads of the same gene in DNA sequencing means higher coverage. With RNA sequencing, it means more copies of the transcript. With bisulfite sequencing, we care about what proportion of the reads at any particular site have a uracil instead of a cytosine.

Accessing data from public databases

If you end up working as a bioinformatician or computational geneticist, you will likely spend a large amount of time gaining access to other people’s datasets in order to replicate your results or gain additional power for statistical analyses. Here are some good places to start looking:

Open access databases

  • TCGA: The Cancer Genome Atlas
  • 1000 Genomes
  • HapMap3
  • NCBI has a variety of databases available, some of which include genetic data
  • GEO: Gene Expression Omnibus

Restricted access databases

Because genetic data can be used to identify individuals, most genetic data is restricted access, especially if it is associated with any phenotypes. Many of these databases are open to anybody with a good question and university credentials, so consider using restricted access data as an opportunity to clarify your ideas before getting too deep into any analyses.

These ambiguity characters were designed to encode positional variations in order to report DNA sequencing errors, consensus sequences, or single-nucleotide polymorphisms.↩︎

  3. Uracil is thymine without a methyl group and replaces thymine in RNA, bonding with adenine. When the International Union of Biochemistry (IUB) and International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) codes were being rewritten in the 1980s, the committee decided that RNA nitrogenous bases were best represented by their DNA counterparts. For this reason, the FASTA files for RNA sequences on NCBI are encoded as their reverse complements (the cDNA). However, you can include uracil (U) in your FASTA files as it was a part of the original 1965 recommendations and BLAST will understand what it means.↩︎

  4. Cytosine, thymine, and uracil have only one nitrogen ring in their structures, making them pyrimidines.↩︎

  5. Adenine and guanine have two nitrogen rings in their structures, making them purines.↩︎

  6. Because of their structures, cytosine and guanine form three hydrogen bonds with each other, which is a stronger interaction than that between adenine and thymine or uracil. This strong interaction is the reason that DNA with high GC-content is more stable.↩︎

  7. Adenine and thymine or uracil form only two hydrogen bonds with each other.↩︎

  8. Guanine, thymine, and uracil are ketones, which means they have at least one carbon-oxygen double bond (carbonyl group). Guanine has one carbonyl group and thymine and uracil have two. While cytosine also has a carbonyl group, this group is the hydrogen bond acceptor rather than the donor.↩︎

  9. Adenine and cytosine have an amino group (NH^2) that hydrogen bonds to the carbonyl group of the ketone bases. Guanine also has an amino group, but - like with cytosine’s ketone group - this amine is the hydrogen bond acceptor, not the donor.↩︎
